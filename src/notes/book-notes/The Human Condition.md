### Chapter 1: *Vita Activa* and the Human Condition

*Vita activa*, the active life, consists of:

- Labor - biological necessities
- Work - what we do and make
- Action - plurality of humankind

Aristotle saw freedom as making possible only three ways of life:

- Pleasure, the hedonistic life
- Politics, the *active* life
- Philosophy, the *contemplative* life

Philosophy > Politics > Pleasure.

Politics is to be virtuous amongst men, and do excellent deeds.

Labor is the life of the slave.
Work is the life of the craftsmen, and the merchant.

To the Greeks,
*Vita* *contemplativa* > *vita activa*

In modern philosophy, Marx and Nietzsche claim
*Vita activa* > *Vita contemplativa*

The Greeks saw not only their gods, but also animals as immortal. Animals are all the same species, and have no distinction, so one is replaced by another.

Only men are mortal. No two men are alike -- they are individuals.

> This individual life is distinguished from all other things by the rectilinear course of its movement, which, so to speak, cuts through the circular movement of biological life.

But men too have a chance at conjuring *immortality* through their works and deeds. This is the basis of the Greek aspiration for greatness, the spring from which *vita activa* flows.

Yet, with the fall of the Roman empire and the rise of Christianity, rose modernity, and whilst we lost immortality, the orphaned *vita* *activa* remained.

*You can just do things*.
